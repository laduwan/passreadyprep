## Chapter 13 — Neurocognitive Disorders

### Overview

Neurocognitive disorders (NCDs) are characterized by a decline in cognitive function from a previous level of performance. The exam tests two key skills: (1) recognizing delirium as a medical emergency distinct from dementia, and (2) understanding the counselor's role in supporting clients and caregivers rather than reversing the condition.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Key Feature | Counselor Role |
|-----------|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Major Neurocognitive Disorder (Dementia) | F02.xx | Significant decline in ≥1 cognitive domain; interferes with independence | Caregiver support, behavioral management, coordination |
| Mild Neurocognitive Disorder | G31.84 | Modest cognitive decline; independence maintained | Psychoeducation, monitoring, lifestyle |
| Delirium | F05 | Acute, fluctuating disturbance in attention and awareness; medical emergency | Refer immediately for medical evaluation |

### The Clinical Picture

**Delirium vs. Dementia:** The most important distinction in this chapter.

| Feature | Delirium | Dementia |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Onset | Acute (hours to days) | Gradual (months to years) |
| Course | Fluctuating | Slowly progressive |
| Attention | Markedly impaired | Relatively preserved early |
| Reversible? | Often yes (treat cause) | Rarely reversible |
| Common causes | Infection, medication, metabolic | Alzheimer's, vascular, Lewy body |

A client who "seemed fine last week and now doesn't know where they are" has delirium until proven otherwise. **Delirium is a medical emergency.** Do not attempt psychotherapy. Refer immediately.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Recognizing delirium and referring for immediate medical evaluation
- Providing grief-informed support to caregivers (caregiver burden is immense)
- Functional assessment to match level of care to current ability
- Using structured, predictable environments and routines for dementia clients

**Punishes:**
- Treating delirium as a behavioral or psychiatric problem
- Attempting insight-oriented therapy with moderate-to-severe dementia clients
- Missing caregiver burnout in the family system

### Practice Question

**Question 41.** An 81-year-old client who has been in counseling for grief presents with his daughter, who reports he has been "confused and agitated since last night" and cannot remember where he is. He is usually oriented and articulate. The counselor should:

A. Administer a cognitive screening tool and schedule a follow-up  
B. Contact his prescribing physician immediately and facilitate emergency evaluation  
C. Provide reassurance and see whether the confusion resolves by the next session  
D. Increase session frequency to monitor cognitive changes  

**Correct: B.** Acute onset confusion in a previously oriented elder is delirium until proven otherwise. Medical emergency. Refer immediately. A, C, and D all delay necessary medical intervention.

## Chapter 14 — Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders

### Overview

OCD and related disorders are characterized by unwanted, repetitive thoughts and/or behaviors that the person cannot stop despite wanting to. The critical exam concept: these disorders are **ego-dystonic** — the client experiences the obsessions or compulsions as unwanted intrusions, not as expressions of their own values. This is what distinguishes OCD from OCPD (which is ego-syntonic).

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Feature | Treatment |
|-----------|--------|-------------|----------|
| OCD | F42.2 | Obsessions (intrusive thoughts) + compulsions (rituals that reduce anxiety) | ERP (Exposure and Response Prevention), medication (SSRI) |
| Body Dysmorphic Disorder | F45.22 | Preoccupation with perceived defect in appearance | CBT/ERP, SSRI |
| Hoarding Disorder | F42.3 | Difficulty discarding possessions + excessive acquisition + cluttered living space | CBT (specialized), motivational interviewing |
| Trichotillomania | F63.3 | Recurrent hair pulling | HRT (Habit Reversal Training) |
| Excoriation (Skin-Picking) | L98.1 | Recurrent skin picking | HRT |

### The Clinical Picture

**OCD** in a case sim: A client describes intrusive thoughts ("What if I hurt someone?", "What if I left the stove on?") that they experience as horrifying and foreign to their values. They engage in rituals — checking, counting, washing, reassurance-seeking — that provide temporary relief but fuel the obsessive cycle in the long run. The checking or washing is not the problem; the problem is the *attempted solution* (the ritual) that prevents habituation to the obsessive thought.

**The OCD cycle:**
Intrusive thought → anxiety spike → compulsion → temporary relief → intrusion returns stronger → repeat

ERP works by breaking the link between the obsession and the compulsion: the client confronts the feared thought without engaging the compulsion, allowing anxiety to naturally reduce through habituation.

**Hoarding Disorder** is often misunderstood. Clients with hoarding disorder form intense attachments to objects, experience severe distress at the prospect of discarding, and may not perceive the hoarding as a problem (making it partially ego-syntonic). CBT for hoarding is specialized and requires motivational enhancement, not just behavioral strategies.

### Differentiating Within the Family

**OCD vs. OCPD:** OCD = ego-dystonic, unwanted obsessions and compulsions. OCPD = ego-syntonic, the person values their orderliness and perfectionism. These can co-occur but they are distinct.

**OCD vs. GAD:** Both involve worry. In OCD, the worries are intrusive, often bizarre or ego-dystonic ("What if I'm secretly a violent person?"), and are accompanied by ritualistic responses. GAD worries are about realistic life concerns (finances, health, relationships) and are not accompanied by compulsions.

**BDD vs. Normal Appearance Concern:** Everyone has appearance concerns. BDD is distinguished by the preoccupation being time-consuming (>1 hour/day), causing significant distress, leading to repetitive behaviors (mirror-checking, excessive grooming, seeking reassurance), and being based on a *perceived* defect that is either absent or minimal. BDD is associated with high rates of suicidality.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- ERP as the first-line treatment for OCD (not CBT without the exposure/response-prevention component)
- Recognizing that compulsions maintain the disorder by providing short-term relief
- HRT (habit reversal training) for trichotillomania and excoriation
- Assessing suicidality in BDD (high risk population)

**Punishes:**
- Using relaxation techniques to manage OCD (reduces anxiety in the moment, reinforces avoidance)
- Treating OCD with standard CBT without the ERP component
- Responding to hoarding disorder by immediately pushing the client to discard items (increases reactance)

### Practice Questions

**Question 42.** A client with OCD describes checking the stove 30–40 times before leaving his apartment. He is 45 minutes late to work most days. He says the checking "has to be done or something terrible will happen." The MOST appropriate evidence-based treatment is:

A. CBT to identify and restructure the catastrophic belief about the stove  
B. Exposure and response prevention: gradually approaching the feared situation (leaving) without performing the ritual (checking)  
C. Progressive muscle relaxation to reduce the anxiety that triggers checking  
D. Mindfulness meditation to create distance from the intrusive thoughts  

**Correct: B.** ERP is the gold-standard treatment for OCD. Cognitive restructuring (A) can be a helpful adjunct but is not sufficient alone. Relaxation (C) reduces anxiety without confronting the rituals — maintaining the disorder. Mindfulness (D) is an adjunct but not the primary treatment.

---

**Question 43.** A 19-year-old woman is referred by her dermatologist after presenting with wounds from picking at her skin for 2–3 hours daily. She describes difficulty stopping and feels distress after picking sessions. She has tried to stop independently multiple times. The counselor should:

A. Diagnose OCD and begin ERP  
B. Diagnose Excoriation Disorder and use Habit Reversal Training as the primary treatment  
C. Assess for self-harm and refer for psychiatric evaluation  
D. Provide psychoeducation about the dermatological risks and encourage willpower  

**Correct: B.** Excoriation (skin-picking) disorder is treated with HRT (habit reversal training) — awareness training, competing response training, and social support. It is distinct from OCD (different disorder, different treatment). Self-harm (C) should be ruled out but the presentation is more consistent with excoriation. D is not evidence-based.

## Chapter 15 — Schizophrenia Spectrum and Other Psychotic Disorders

### Overview

Schizophrenia spectrum disorders are among the most severe on the NCMHCE, and the exam tests them differently from most other chapters — less about therapeutic interventions you would personally deliver, more about recognition, appropriate referral, and collaborative care. A key exam principle: **individual psychotherapy is not the primary treatment for active psychosis.** The counselor's role includes case management, psychoeducation (for client and family), and coordination with the psychiatric treatment team.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Duration | Key Features |
|-----------|--------|---------|-------------|
| Schizophrenia | F20.9 | ≥6 months (active symptoms ≥1 month) | ≥2 of: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, disorganized behavior, negative symptoms |
| Schizophreniform Disorder | F20.81 | 1–6 months | Same symptoms; good premorbid functioning is a positive prognostic indicator |
| Brief Psychotic Disorder | F23 | 1 day – 1 month | Sudden onset, often following stressor; full recovery expected |
| Schizoaffective Disorder | F25.x | Duration variable | Meets criteria for schizophrenia + concurrent mood episode; mood episode present for majority of illness |
| Delusional Disorder | F22 | ≥1 month | Non-bizarre delusions; relatively intact functioning outside the delusion |

### The Clinical Picture

**Positive symptoms** (things added to experience): hallucinations (hearing, seeing, feeling, smelling things not there), delusions (fixed false beliefs not culturally explained), disorganized speech (loosening of associations, word salad), and disorganized behavior.

**Negative symptoms** (things removed from experience): flat affect, alogia (poverty of speech), avolition (lack of motivation), anhedonia, social withdrawal. Negative symptoms are harder to treat and more predictive of long-term functional impairment.

In a case simulation, recognize psychosis by: the client reports experiences no one else can verify (hearing voices, being watched by the government), the thinking pattern is tangential or incoherent, the client's behavior is bizarre or self-neglecting, and the client shows flat or inappropriate affect.

The counselor's role when a client presents with active psychosis: **do not engage with the content of delusions.** Neither agree ("Yes, the government may be watching you") nor argue ("That's not real"). Maintain calm, focused, reality-grounded interaction. Refer for psychiatric evaluation and medication management. The antipsychotic medications are the primary treatment.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Immediate psychiatric referral for active psychosis
- Family psychoeducation (Family-focused therapy / NAMI involvement) to reduce expressed emotion (EE), which predicts relapse
- Supported employment and cognitive remediation in the recovery/maintenance phase
- Recognizing negative symptoms as distinct from depression

**Punishes:**
- Attempting insight-oriented individual therapy during active psychosis
- Arguing with delusional content
- Prescribing or managing antipsychotics (outside the counselor's scope)
- Missing the medical rule-out (neurological causes, substance-induced psychosis)

### Practice Question

**Question 44.** A client who has been stable on antipsychotic medication tells her counselor she has stopped taking her medication because "the pills are part of the control system." The counselor's MOST appropriate response is:

A. Explore the client's concerns about the medication in a non-confrontational manner and contact the prescribing psychiatrist  
B. Reassure her that medication is safe and will not harm her  
C. Discharge the client as she is no longer treatment-compliant  
D. Hospitalize the client immediately due to the likelihood of relapse  

**Correct: A.** The counselor explores the concern (does not argue with the delusion) and coordinates with the prescriber. This is the collaborative care model. B argues against the delusional belief. C abandons the client at a critical moment. D may eventually be appropriate but is not the immediate first step.

## Chapter 16 — Feeding and Eating Disorders

### Overview

Eating disorders carry the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric condition — a statistic the NCMHCE exam knows and tests. The exam expects you to: assess medical stability first, know the diagnostic criteria, apply the appropriate level of care, and understand the multi-team nature of eating disorder treatment.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Feature | Medical Concern |
|-----------|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Anorexia Nervosa — Restricting | F50.01 | Restriction, low weight, body image disturbance, fear of gaining | Cardiac arrhythmia, bone loss, lanugo, bradycardia — HIGH mortality |
| Anorexia Nervosa — Binge/Purge | F50.02 | Restriction + binge/purge cycles, low weight | Same as above |
| Bulimia Nervosa | F50.2 | Binge-purge cycles, normal or above-average weight, shame cycle | Electrolyte imbalance, enamel erosion, Russell's sign |
| Binge Eating Disorder | F50.81 | Recurrent binge episodes without compensatory behaviors; significant distress | Obesity-related metabolic effects |
| Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder (ARFID) | F50.82 | Restriction not driven by body image; sensory, fear, or disinterest-based | Nutritional deficiency, growth failure in children |
| Pica | F98.3 | Eating non-nutritive substances | Lead poisoning, GI obstruction |
| Rumination Disorder | F98.21 | Repeated regurgitation of food | Nutritional, dental, social |

### The Clinical Picture

**Anorexia Nervosa (AN):** The client presents as underweight but may not perceive themselves as thin — body image disturbance is a core feature. They may be brought in by family (the client often does not self-refer because the disorder is ego-syntonic). They may minimize the problem ("I eat fine") or be engaged but hide restriction severity. Physical signs: lanugo (fine hair on face/body), bradycardia, cold intolerance, amenorrhea, dental erosion (in binge/purge type).

**Bulimia Nervosa (BN):** The binge-purge cycle is driven by shame. The client often eats normally in public and purges privately. They are more likely than AN clients to self-refer because the disorder is ego-dystonic — the binges and purges feel out of control and shameful. Russell's sign (calluses on knuckles from self-induced vomiting) is a physical marker.

**The Refeeding Syndrome:** A potentially fatal electrolyte shift that can occur when malnourished clients (particularly AN) begin eating again too quickly. It is a medical emergency. Medical monitoring is essential during refeeding.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Medical evaluation FIRST for any suspected eating disorder (cardiac monitoring, electrolytes, bone density)
- Knowing that higher levels of care (residential, partial hospital) are required for medically unstable clients
- Multi-disciplinary team approach (counselor + dietitian + physician + psychiatrist)
- Family-Based Treatment (FBT/Maudsley approach) as first-line for adolescents with AN
- CBT-E (enhanced) as evidence-based treatment for BN and BED

**Punishes:**
- Attempting outpatient therapy with a medically unstable client with AN
- Focusing only on weight restoration without addressing the psychological maintaining factors
- Colluding with the client's body image distortion (e.g., agreeing that they "look a little heavy")
- Missing that purging in BN can cause fatal electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia → cardiac arrhythmia)

### Worked Mini-Case

*Intake vignette:*
Sophia, 16, is brought in by her parents. She has lost 22 pounds over the past 8 months. Her parents became alarmed when her pulse at a school screening was 48 bpm. She says she is "eating fine" and doesn't understand why everyone is worried. She denies purging. Her BMI is 16.2.

**Reasoning chain:**

Step 1 — Medical emergency: Bradycardia (48 bpm) in an underweight adolescent with significant weight loss is a cardiac warning sign. Before any psychotherapy, medical evaluation is needed. Sophia may need hospitalization for medical stabilization.

Step 2 — Level of care: Based on the ASAM or eating disorder-specific criteria (MEDA: medical stability, eating disorder behaviors, BMI, psychiatric comorbidities), Sophia may need residential or inpatient medical level before outpatient counseling.

Step 3 — Denial: Her insistence that she is "eating fine" reflects the ego-syntonic quality and body image distortion of AN. The counselor does not argue but maintains the medical priority.

Step 4 — Treatment once medically stable: Family-Based Treatment (FBT/Maudsley) is the first-line for adolescents with AN — parents are empowered to temporarily take control of eating while the adolescent's brain is malnourished and unable to make rational food decisions.

### Practice Questions

**Question 45.** A client with bulimia nervosa is engaging in CBT. She reports that last week she binged five times — up from two the previous week. She attributes the increase to exam stress. The counselor should:

A. Review the binge-purge logs and increase CBT session frequency  
B. Evaluate electrolyte levels and physical status, then address the stressor in treatment  
C. Add a relaxation component to address the exam-related stress  
D. Maintain the current treatment without change, as fluctuations are expected  

**Correct: B.** Medical safety is always rung 2. An increase in purging frequency increases the risk of hypokalemia (from vomiting or laxative use) — electrolyte evaluation is warranted. The stressor can then be integrated into CBT work. A skips the medical check. C and D are insufficient responses to an acute escalation.

**Question 46.** Which of the following presentations warrants IMMEDIATE medical evaluation or hospitalization rather than outpatient counseling?

A. A client with bulimia nervosa who has been purging 3 times per week for 6 months, maintains normal BMI, and is motivated for treatment  
B. A client with binge eating disorder who has gained 30 pounds and has comorbid depression  
C. A client with anorexia nervosa presenting with BMI of 14.5, heart rate of 44 bpm, and reports of fainting  
D. A client with ARFID who limits food textures but maintains adequate nutrition and growth  

**Correct: C.** BMI 14.5 (severely low), bradycardia (44 bpm), and syncopal episodes indicate medical instability. This client requires immediate medical evaluation and likely inpatient or residential medical level of care before any outpatient therapy begins. ## Chapter 17 — Crisis, Risk, and Safety

### Overview

This chapter is non-negotiable. Every NCMHCE candidate must have a thorough, fluent understanding of risk assessment and crisis response — because safety (rung 1) applies to every single case simulation that touches on suicidality, homicidality, abuse, or danger. No clinical action precedes safety assessment when risk is present.

The exam tests two specific things about suicidality: (1) your ability to conduct a thorough, direct, and compassionate risk assessment, and (2) your ability to match the appropriate level of care to the assessed risk level.

### The Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS) Framework

The C-SSRS is the most widely used structured risk assessment tool and the framework the exam implicitly uses. It assesses:

**Ideation:**
- Wish to be dead (passive)
- Active suicidal ideation — nonspecific (without plan or intent)
- Active suicidal ideation with plan
- Active suicidal ideation with plan and intent to act
- Active suicidal ideation with plan, intent, and preparatory behaviors

**Behavior (recent and lifetime):**
- Aborted attempt
- Interrupted attempt
- Actual attempt
- Non-suicidal self-injury (important to distinguish)

### The SAL Framework for Risk Assessment

**S — Suicidal Ideation:** Presence, frequency, intensity, duration. Active vs. passive.

**A — Access to means:** Does the client have a gun at home? Pills available? Access to lethal means dramatically increases risk. **Means restriction counseling** — reducing access to the most lethal means — is a primary safety intervention with high evidence.

**L — Lethality of plan:** A plan involving firearms is higher risk than a plan involving pills. A specific plan with a time, place, and method is higher risk than vague ideation.

**Additional risk factors:**
- Prior attempt (strongest single predictor of future attempt)
- Current substance intoxication
- Recent significant loss
- Social isolation / lack of support
- History of impulsivity
- Male sex (higher completion rate); female sex (higher attempt rate)
- Access to means

**Protective factors:**
- Reasons for living (children, religious beliefs, future plans)
- Social support
- Help-seeking behavior (client is in your office)
- Engaged in treatment

### Level of Care Matching

| Risk Level | Features | Appropriate Action |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| Low | Passive ideation, no plan, protective factors present, no history of attempts | Safety plan, increase session frequency, remove means |
| Moderate | Active ideation, vague plan, some protective factors, prior attempt history | Safety plan, family/support involvement, possible voluntary hospitalization; possibly intensive outpatient |
| High | Active ideation with specific plan, intent, means available, prior attempt, intoxicated | Emergency psychiatric evaluation; 5150/involuntary hold if imminent danger |
| Imminent | States intent to act immediately; has means | Call 911 / contact emergency services; do not leave client alone |

### The Safety Plan

A safety plan (not a "no-harm contract" — the research does not support no-harm contracts as effective) includes:
1. Warning signs (what the client notices before crisis escalates)
2. Internal coping strategies (what the client can do alone)
3. Social contacts for distraction (friends/family who don't need to know the reason)
4. People and agencies to contact for help (with numbers)
5. Making the environment safer (restricting means)
6. Reasons for living

### Mandated Reporting: Child Abuse and Neglect

All mental health counselors are mandated reporters. When there is *reasonable suspicion* (not certainty) of child abuse or neglect, the report must be made to Child Protective Services. The counselor does not investigate; the counselor reports.

**Four types of child maltreatment:**
- Physical abuse
- Sexual abuse
- Emotional/psychological abuse
- Neglect (by far the most common)

**Duty to Warn (Tarasoff):** When a client makes a specific, credible threat against an identifiable third party, the counselor has a duty to protect that third party. Actions may include: warning the intended victim, notifying law enforcement, or arranging for involuntary hospitalization. The specific obligations vary by state. The exam tests the principle — specific, identifiable threat, identifiable victim — not state-specific law.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Direct, compassionate, non-judgmental suicidal ideation assessment ("Are you having thoughts of suicide?")
- Using the safety plan over the no-harm contract
- Means restriction counseling
- Matching level of care to risk (not automatically hospitalizing every suicidal client, but not under-responding to high-risk presentations)
- Reporting suspected abuse when there is reasonable suspicion — not waiting for certainty

**Punishes:**
- Avoiding direct inquiry about suicidal ideation ("I didn't want to put the idea in their head" — this is a myth)
- Assuming chronic suicidality in BPD doesn't need ongoing assessment
- Failing to report suspected abuse due to fear of disrupting the therapeutic alliance
- Using a no-harm contract as a substitute for a thorough risk assessment
- Discharging a high-risk client without a safety plan

### Worked Mini-Case

*Vignette:*
A client calls between sessions and says, "I've been thinking about taking all my sleeping pills tonight. I have them right here. I just don't see a way out."

**Reasoning chain:**

This is a high-to-imminent risk presentation:
- Active ideation ✓
- Specific plan (sleeping pills) ✓
- Access to means (has them available) ✓
- Intent (tonight) ✓
- Hopelessness ("I don't see a way out") ✓

Response sequence:
1. Stay on the line; do not hang up.
2. Ask her to move away from the pills while speaking with you.
3. Assess whether she has taken anything already (medical emergency if yes → 911).
4. Engage her around reasons for living; explore what led to the escalation tonight.
5. Arrange for emergency evaluation: either the client agrees to go to the ER (and a support person accompanies her), or if she refuses and the risk is imminent, call 911 with her on the phone or with knowledge of her address.
6. Do not end the call until a safety plan is in place or emergency services have been contacted.

### Practice Questions

**Question 47.** A client discloses active suicidal ideation with a specific plan to hang himself using a rope he has already purchased. He denies intent to act "tonight" but says "soon." He lives alone. The counselor should:

A. Create a safety plan and schedule a session for tomorrow  
B. Conduct a full risk assessment, remove the means if possible, and arrange emergency psychiatric evaluation  
C. Contact his family to stay with him tonight and follow up next session  
D. Administer the PHQ-9 and document the disclosure in the chart  

**Correct: B.** This is high-risk: specific plan, purchased means, lives alone, intent expressed as "soon." Emergency psychiatric evaluation is required. A safety plan alone is insufficient at this risk level. Family contact (C) may be part of the plan but not the *primary* action. Documentation (D) is necessary but not a safety intervention.

A. Acknowledge the statement and continue the session, as chronic suicidality is expected in BPD

## Chapter 18 — Disruptive, Impulse-Control, and Conduct Disorders

### Overview

This family groups disorders characterized by problems with self-regulation of emotions and behavior that violate the rights of others or bring the person into conflict with social norms. The exam primarily tests this in the context of youth populations and asks you to distinguish between childhood behavioral presentations.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Key Feature | Age/Context |
|-----------|--------|-------------|------------|
| Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD) | F91.3 | Angry/irritable mood + argumentative/defiant behavior + vindictiveness ≥6 months | Children/adolescents; directed at authority figures |
| Conduct Disorder | F91.x | Serious violation of others' rights and societal norms (aggression, destruction, deceit, rule violations) | Children/adolescents; precursor to ASPD |
| Intermittent Explosive Disorder | F63.81 | Recurrent, impulsive aggressive outbursts disproportionate to provocation | Adolescents and adults |
| Antisocial PD | F60.2 | Adult pattern of rights violations (requires CD before 15) | Adults ≥18 |
| Pyromania | F63.1 | Deliberate fire-setting with fascination and tension-relief pattern | Rare |
| Kleptomania | F63.2 | Recurrent stealing not for gain; tension-relief pattern | Rare |

### The Clinical Picture

**ODD vs. CD:** The critical distinction is severity and type of rule violation. ODD involves defiance, arguing, and irritability — annoying but not seriously harmful. CD involves aggression toward people or animals, destruction of property, deceit/theft, and serious rule violations. A child who argues with teachers and refuses chores has ODD; a child who fights, breaks into homes, and tortures animals has CD.

**The developmental pathway:** ODD → CD → ASPD is a known risk trajectory, especially with early-onset CD and lack of early intervention. Not all ODD children develop CD, and not all CD adolescents develop ASPD — but the association is clinically important and the exam tests it.

**Parenting components:** Evidence-based treatment for ODD and CD includes parent management training (PMT). Parents are taught to reinforce prosocial behavior consistently, set clear and enforceable limits, use natural consequences, and reduce inadvertent reinforcement of problem behavior.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Parent management training as the core treatment for ODD/CD in children
- Functional behavioral assessment to understand the ABCs of the behavior (antecedent → behavior → consequence)
- Multisystemic therapy (MST) for severe adolescent CD (targets family, peer, school, community systems simultaneously)
- Assessing for co-occurring ADHD, trauma, or mood disorders (ODD rarely appears alone)

**Punishes:**
- Individual insight-oriented therapy as the sole treatment for ODD/CD (insufficient without systemic components)
- Diagnosing CD or ASPD in young children (ASPD requires ≥18 and prior CD)
- Failing to assess the family system's contribution to the behavioral pattern

### Practice Question

**Question 51.** A 9-year-old boy is referred for aggressive behavior. He has been suspended three times this year for fighting, was caught setting a small fire in the schoolyard, and his parents report he has stolen money from them multiple times. These behaviors have persisted for over a year. The MOST appropriate diagnosis to consider is:

A. ADHD — predominantly hyperactive/impulsive  
B. ODD  
C. Conduct Disorder  
D. Intermittent Explosive Disorder  

**Correct: C.** Aggression toward others, fire-setting, and theft are CD criteria — these are serious violations of others' rights, not just defiant behavior (ODD). ADHD may co-occur but doesn't account for the pattern of rights violations. IED involves impulsive aggression but not the deceit and rule-violation pattern.

## Chapter 19 — Somatic Symptom and Related Disorders

### Overview

Somatic symptom and related disorders involve physical symptoms that cause significant distress or impairment — but the central issue is the *psychological response* to those symptoms (excessive worry, disproportionate fear) rather than whether the symptoms are medically explained. The DSM-5 removed the concept of "medically unexplained symptoms" as a requirement — even a client with a diagnosed medical condition can have a somatic symptom disorder if their response to the symptoms is excessive and disabling.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Feature |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Somatic Symptom Disorder | F45.1 | ≥1 somatic symptom with excessive thoughts, feelings, or behaviors related to the symptom |
| Illness Anxiety Disorder | F45.21 | Fear of having a serious illness with minimal or no somatic symptoms |
| Conversion Disorder (Functional Neurological Symptom Disorder) | F44.x | Motor or sensory symptoms that cannot be explained neurologically |
| Factitious Disorder | F68.1x | Falsification of medical/psychological symptoms; motivated by sick role |
| Psychological Factors Affecting Medical Condition | F54 | Psychological factors adversely affect a medical condition |

### Differentiating Within the Family

**Somatic Symptom Disorder vs. Illness Anxiety Disorder:** The key distinction: SSD has significant somatic symptoms with excessive psychological response to them. IAD has minimal or absent somatic symptoms with intense fear of having a serious illness (previously called hypochondriasis in DSM-IV).

**Conversion Disorder vs. Malingering vs. Factitious Disorder:**
- Conversion: Symptoms are real to the client; not consciously produced; no external incentive
- Factitious: Symptoms are consciously produced; motivated by the sick role (internal incentive)
- Malingering: Symptoms are consciously produced; motivated by external incentive (avoiding legal consequences, getting disability payments)

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Medical evaluation first (even in somatic disorders, a medical workup establishes the clinical picture)
- Avoiding dismissive language ("it's all in your head" — never appropriate)
- CBT targeting catastrophic health beliefs and hypervigilance to bodily sensations
- Maintaining the alliance with clients who have been dismissed by medical providers

**Punishes:**
- Diagnosing somatic symptom disorder before completing a medical evaluation
- Treating somatic disorders without addressing the psychological response patterns
- Colluding with excessive medical testing as a reassurance behavior

### Practice Question

**Question 52.** A client presents reporting persistent chest pain, dizziness, and shortness of breath that have been evaluated by three different cardiologists with no medical findings. She spends 4–5 hours daily researching her symptoms online and visits the ER every two weeks. She is increasingly unable to work. The MOST appropriate clinical response is:

A. Refer to a fourth specialist, as the medical evaluation may have been incomplete  
B. Diagnose conversion disorder and begin CBT  
C. Acknowledge the distress caused by her symptoms, affirm the medical clearances, and begin CBT targeting health anxiety behaviors  
D. Refer to a psychiatrist for medication to address anxiety symptoms  

**Correct: C.** This presentation is consistent with Somatic Symptom Disorder or Illness Anxiety Disorder — the physical symptoms are real to her; the response (5 hours of online research, frequent ER visits) is disproportionate and maintaining the anxiety. CBT targeting reassurance-seeking and health anxiety is the evidence-based treatment. A reinforces excessive help-seeking. B misidentifies the diagnosis (conversion involves neurological symptoms). D is not first-line.

## Chapter 20 — Dissociative Disorders

### Overview

Dissociative disorders involve disruptions in consciousness, memory, identity, or perception. They are almost always rooted in trauma and are among the most misunderstood and stigmatized diagnoses in mental health. The exam tests recognition and appropriate treatment planning — primarily the three-phase trauma treatment model and the stabilization-before-processing principle (which is especially critical in DID).

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Feature |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID) | F44.81 | ≥2 distinct personality states/identities; amnesia between states |
| Dissociative Amnesia | F44.0 | Inability to recall important autobiographical information (not ordinary forgetting) |
| Depersonalization/Derealization Disorder | F48.1 | Persistent experiences of unreality or detachment from self (depersonalization) or surroundings (derealization) |

### The Clinical Picture

**DID** in a case sim: A client reports "losing time," being told they did or said things they don't remember, finding objects or writings they don't recall creating, hearing voices "from inside" (not hallucinations in the schizophrenic sense — internal voices belonging to other identity states), and extreme identity confusion. The voices are often experienced as distinctly different from the client's own thoughts.

**Depersonalization/derealization:** The client describes feeling like they are watching themselves from outside their body (depersonalization) or that the world looks unreal, foggy, or dreamlike (derealization). These experiences are disturbing and persistent. Common in panic disorder, PTSD, and trauma histories, but when they are the persistent, primary complaint, the disorder is DP/DR disorder.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- The three-phase trauma model (stabilization → processing → integration) — applied even more carefully in DID because processing without stabilization can cause rapid destabilization between identity states
- Compassionate, non-reactive acceptance of DID presentations (not treating the diagnosis as inherently dramatic or invalid)
- Assessment of safety for *all* identity states in DID (some states may be suicidal even if the presenting state is not)

**Punishes:**
- Attempting direct trauma processing with DID clients who lack sufficient stabilization and communication between states
- Dismissing dissociative symptoms as manipulation
- Failing to recognize that reported "hearing voices" in DID clients is internal (identity-based) rather than external hallucination

### Practice Question

**Question 53.** A client with Dissociative Identity Disorder has been in treatment for four months. She is now requesting to begin EMDR to process childhood abuse memories. The counselor should:

A. Begin EMDR immediately, as the client has been in treatment long enough  
B. Assess the current level of stabilization: communication between states, capacity to stay grounded, availability of coping skills; proceed to EMDR only if stabilization criteria are met  
C. Refuse EMDR and maintain supportive treatment only  
D. Increase session frequency to support the client through the EMDR process  

**Correct: B.** Phase 1 (stabilization) criteria must be met before Phase 2 (processing) begins — this is even more critical in DID where destabilization can occur rapidly and different identity states may process trauma differently. A ignores phase criteria. C withholds an evidence-based treatment unnecessarily. D adds sessions without addressing the phase readiness question.

## Chapter 21 — Sleep-Wake Disorders

### Overview

Sleep disorders appear on the NCMHCE primarily as co-occurring conditions that complicate other diagnoses. A counselor who misses that poor sleep is driving cognitive impairment, irritability, or mood instability will misattribute symptoms to a mood or anxiety disorder. The exam expects you to assess sleep as a routine component of the intake.

### Key Sleep Disorders for the NCMHCE

| Disorder | Core Feature | Counselor Role |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| Insomnia Disorder | Difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep ≥3 nights/week, ≥3 months | CBT-I (first-line over medication) |
| Hypersomnolence Disorder | Excessive sleepiness despite adequate sleep | Medical evaluation, rule out medical causes |
| Narcolepsy | Recurrent lapses into sleep + cataplexy | Medical management; counselor provides psychoeducation/support |
| Obstructive Sleep Apnea | Repeated airway obstruction during sleep; snoring, daytime fatigue | Medical referral for sleep study, CPAP |
| Nightmare Disorder | Repeated, disturbing dreams causing distress; awakening with recall | IRT (Imagery Rehearsal Therapy), especially if trauma-related |
| REM Sleep Behavior Disorder | Acting out dreams physically (punching, kicking) | Medical evaluation; often prodromal to neurodegenerative disease |

### CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia)

CBT-I is the gold-standard, first-line treatment for chronic insomnia — superior to medication for long-term outcomes. Components:

1. **Sleep restriction therapy** — limit time in bed to actual sleep time to build sleep drive
2. **Stimulus control** — the bed is for sleep and sex only; get up if not asleep within 20 minutes
3. **Sleep hygiene education** — consistent wake time, no screens before bed, cool dark room
4. **Cognitive restructuring** — address catastrophic beliefs about sleep ("If I don't sleep, tomorrow is ruined")
5. **Relaxation training** — progressive muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing

### Practice Question

**Question 54.** A client with insomnia reports she has been taking a sleep aid for 6 months and wants to reduce the medication. She often lies awake in bed for 2–3 hours and has begun watching television in bed. The MOST appropriate evidence-based intervention is:

A. Refer to a sleep specialist for a sleep study  
B. Support the gradual medication taper and add CBT-I: sleep restriction and stimulus control  
C. Recommend that she use the bedroom for sleep only and introduce a consistent wake time without medication changes  
D. Provide relaxation training only, as that addresses the hyperarousal component  

**Correct: B.** CBT-I (specifically sleep restriction and stimulus control, which addresses the bed-watching behavior) combined with a supported medication taper is the evidence-based approach. C is partially correct (stimulus control, wake time) but the medication taper is an important concurrent goal. D addresses only one component of CBT-I.

## Chapter 22 — Sexual Dysfunctions and Gender Dysphoria

### Overview

This chapter tests two distinct topic areas: (1) sexual dysfunctions — problems with desire, arousal, or orgasm that cause significant distress — and (2) gender dysphoria — distress arising from incongruence between gender identity and assigned sex. The exam's approach to gender dysphoria reflects an affirming model: the counselor's role is to support the client's self-identified gender, explore the distress, and facilitate access to care — not to question, challenge, or attempt to change the client's gender identity.

### Sexual Dysfunctions Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Feature |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Female Sexual Interest/Arousal Disorder | F52.22 | Reduced or absent interest in/arousal to sexual activity |
| Female Orgasmic Disorder | F52.31 | Difficulty or inability to achieve orgasm |
| Genito-Pelvic Pain/Penetration Disorder | F52.6 | Difficulty with penetration; pelvic floor tension/pain; fear |
| Male Hypoactive Sexual Desire Disorder | F52.0 | Persistently reduced/absent sexual desire |
| Erectile Disorder | F52.21 | Difficulty achieving/maintaining erection |
| Delayed/Absent Ejaculation | F52.32 | Marked delay or absence of ejaculation |
| Premature Ejaculation | F52.4 | Ejaculation within ~1 minute of penetration, consistently |

All sexual dysfunctions require: persistent, recurring, causes significant distress to the individual, and is not better explained by a non-sexual mental disorder, substance, medication, or medical condition.

**Medical rule-out is essential:** Many sexual dysfunctions have organic causes (hormonal, vascular, neurological, medication side effects). The counselor should always coordinate with a medical provider.

### Gender Dysphoria

**DSM-5 criteria:** Marked incongruence between the person's experienced/expressed gender and assigned gender, lasting ≥6 months, causing significant distress or functional impairment.

**Affirming stance:** The NCMHCE expects counselors to use the client's preferred pronouns and name, validate gender identity as real and valid, assess and support the client's goals (which may include social transition, medical transition, or neither), and address the distress caused by social rejection, discrimination, or dysphoria — not attempt to align the client's gender identity with their assigned sex.

**Counselor role:** Psychoeducation, support, advocacy, coordination with medical providers for clients seeking hormonal or surgical intervention (informed consent model or WPATH Standards of Care model), and mental health assessment — not gatekeeper, but collaborative partner.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Medical evaluation for sexual dysfunctions before initiating psychological treatment
- Affirming stance with gender-diverse clients (not questioning the validity of their identity)
- Assessment of distress source in gender dysphoria (is it the incongruence itself, or the social/family rejection around it?)

**Punishes:**
- Attempting conversion practices or attempting to change gender identity (clinically and ethically harmful)
- Diagnosing sexual dysfunction without ruling out medical and pharmacological causes
- Pathologizing gender diversity (non-conforming gender expression without distress is not a disorder)

### Practice Question

**Question 55.** A 17-year-old assigned female at birth presents with significant distress and says, "I have never felt like a girl. I feel like I'm in the wrong body, and my parents don't believe me." The counselor's MOST appropriate initial response is:

A. Recommend waiting until age 18 before discussing gender identity concerns  
B. Explore the client's experience with an affirming stance, assess distress, and provide a safe space for self-expression  
C. Refer to a psychiatrist to assess for an underlying psychotic disorder before engaging with gender concerns  
D. Contact the parents to discuss the importance of their support in the client's mental health treatment  

**Correct: B.** An affirming, exploratory, compassionate response is the appropriate starting point. The counselor validates the client's experience and assesses the distress. A is not a clinical or ethical basis for deferring care. C pathologizes gender dysphoria (it is not a psychotic disorder). D may eventually involve the parents, but the immediate response to the client's disclosure is affirming engagement — not immediate parental contact.

## Chapter 23 — Ethical and Legal Decision Points

### Overview

Ethics is not a separate chapter on the NCMHCE — it is woven through every simulation. This chapter synthesizes the ethical principles, legal obligations, and decision-making frameworks that the exam tests in context. The exam never asks you to recite an ethical code; it tests ethical reasoning under realistic clinical pressure.

**The Five Core Ethical Principles (Beauchamp and Childress):**

| Principle | Meaning | Clinical Translation |
|-----------|---------|---------------------|
| Autonomy | The client's right to self-determination | Informed consent, voluntary participation, respecting client decisions |
| Beneficence | Act in the client's best interest | Choose interventions with the best evidence; prioritize client welfare |
| Nonmaleficence | Do no harm | Avoid harmful practices; don't exploit; manage dual relationships carefully |
| Justice | Fair and equal treatment | Nondiscrimination; equitable access to services |
| Fidelity | Honoring commitments; being trustworthy | Confidentiality, follow through on agreements, honest communication |

**ACA Code of Ethics — Most-Tested Areas:**

**Confidentiality and its limits:**
- Confidentiality is the rule; exceptions are the exam's focus
- Exceptions: imminent danger to self or others (duty to warn/protect), abuse of a child/elder/dependent adult (mandated reporting), court order, client waives confidentiality, consultation (limited disclosure), group/family (special considerations)

**Informed consent:** Before treatment begins, clients must be informed of: nature and purpose of treatment, risks and benefits, alternatives, counselor's credentials, fees, confidentiality and its limits, and how to file a complaint. Informed consent is ongoing (not a one-time form).

**Dual relationships:** A dual relationship exists when the counselor has a professional role AND another role with the same client (social, familial, financial, romantic). The ACA prohibits relationships that harm the client's welfare or impair the counselor's objectivity. The exam tests the most common violations: sexual contact with clients (always prohibited, including after termination for a specified period), bartering when not in the client's interest, social media contact, self-disclosure that serves the counselor rather than the client.

**Competence:** Counselors practice only within their areas of competence. When a client presents with needs outside the counselor's competence, the obligation is to refer, consult, or seek additional training. The exam tests whether you recognize the limits of competence and act accordingly.

**Record-keeping and privacy (HIPAA):** Mental health records are protected health information. Release requires written authorization (with specific exceptions). Notes of a personal nature ("psychotherapy notes" under HIPAA) have heightened protection.

**Termination ethics:** Counselors may not abandon clients. Termination must be planned, with referrals provided and adequate notice given. Abandonment — stopping treatment without planning — is an ethical violation. The counselor may terminate when the client is no longer benefiting, the relationship is harmful, or the client poses a risk to the counselor — but must do so with appropriate referral and planning.

### The Ethical Decision-Making Model

When an ethical dilemma arises on the NCMHCE, apply this sequence:

1. **Identify the dilemma:** What are the competing obligations?
2. **Consult the code:** What does the ACA Code say?
3. **Apply ethical principles:** Which principle is most relevant?
4. **Consider consequences:** What are the likely outcomes of each option?
5. **Consult:** With a supervisor or colleague (this is almost always the right answer when uncertain)
6. **Document:** The reasoning, the decision, and the outcome

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Consulting with a supervisor when uncertain about ethical decisions
- Taking a conservative approach to confidentiality (default to protect)
- Recognizing dual relationship risks and acting proactively to maintain boundaries
- Reporting abuse based on reasonable suspicion, not certainty
- Providing referrals when terminating

**Punishes:**
- Acting on a duty-to-warn breach without first attempting to address the risk within the therapeutic relationship (where clinically possible)
- Assuming HIPAA releases cover psychotherapy notes (they have separate protection)
- Providing only a "no-harm contract" without a risk assessment as an ethical response to suicidality
- Maintaining a relationship the counselor knows has become exploitative because "the client wants to continue"

### Practice Questions

**Question 56.** A client discloses that she has been hitting her 6-year-old son "more than I should" because "I just lose it sometimes." She asks the counselor to keep this confidential. The counselor should:

A. Honor the request for confidentiality, as the disclosure was made in a therapeutic context  
B. Assess the severity of the physical discipline and report if it meets the threshold for abuse  
C. Immediately report the disclosure to child protective services based on reasonable suspicion  
D. Discuss the disclosure with a supervisor before deciding whether to report  

**Correct: C.** Physical discipline that rises to "more than I should" and "losing it" constitutes reasonable suspicion of physical abuse. Mandated reporting does not require certainty, and it is not delayed by the request for confidentiality (confidentiality does not protect abuse). B implies the counselor determines the abuse threshold — the role is to report the suspicion, not to investigate. D may be a concurrent step but does not delay reporting.

**Question 57.** A counselor is offered baked goods by a client as a thank-you gift at the end of their last session. The MOST appropriate response is:

A. Decline the gift, as all gifts create dual relationship concerns  
B. Accept the gift with gratitude, as this is the final session and the gesture is appropriate  
C. Consider the value of the gift, the cultural context, and the clinical significance before deciding how to respond  
D. Accept the gift only if the client consents in writing  

**Correct: C.** The ACA Code does not prohibit all gifts — it requires considering the clinical and cultural context. A small gift at termination may have significant cultural meaning. A rigid refusal could harm the alliance and cultural relationship at a significant clinical moment. B accepts without any ethical reflection. D is not an actual ACA requirement.

**Question 58.** A counselor has been seeing a client for individual therapy for two years. The client's spouse contacts the counselor and requests a phone appointment "to discuss my husband's treatment." The counselor should:

A. Schedule the call, as family involvement is clinically valuable  
B. Decline the contact without the client's written authorization for this specific communication  
C. Contact the client and let him decide whether the spouse may speak with the counselor  
D. Provide general information about the client's progress without disclosing specific session content  

**Correct: B.** The counselor cannot speak with the spouse about the client's treatment without the client's explicit, written authorization. The client's name alone being known to the spouse does not constitute a waiver. C is better than A or D but is technically insufficient — authorization must be documented in writing before any contact.

**Question 59.** A client in distress calls after hours and says she needs to speak to the counselor immediately. The counselor has an established after-hours protocol that includes a crisis line number and a clear policy that after-hours contact is for emergencies only. The client says, "This isn't an emergency — I just feel really alone." The counselor should:

A. Speak with the client as long as necessary, as her distress is real  
B. Affirm the client's feelings, remind her of the after-hours protocol and crisis resources, and confirm the next appointment  
C. Decline to speak and direct her immediately to the crisis line  
D. End the call and document the contact in the record  

**Correct: B.** The counselor acknowledges the distress (affirming), maintains the frame (after-hours policy), provides safety resources (crisis line), and bridges to the next session. A dissolves the therapeutic frame. C is abrupt and clinically inadequate. D is incomplete (no acknowledgment of the client's state).

**Question 60.** A client on the counselor's caseload becomes a friend of the counselor's spouse. They begin attending the same social events. The counselor should:

A. Immediately terminate the therapeutic relationship and refer  
B. Discuss the situation with the client, assess the impact on the therapeutic relationship, consult with a supervisor, and document the process  
C. Continue treatment without disclosure, as social proximity does not constitute a dual relationship  
D. Request that the client not attend social events the counselor attends  

**Correct: B.** The emergence of a social proximity dual relationship requires deliberate navigation: disclosure to the client, assessment of impact, consultation, and documentation. The outcome (continue vs. refer) depends on the clinical context and the client's wishes. Immediate termination (A) is not automatically required. Ignoring it (C) is an ethical failure. D is inappropriate and intrusive of the client's social autonomy.
