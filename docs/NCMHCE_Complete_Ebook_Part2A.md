## Chapter 6 — Depressive Disorders

### Overview

Depressive disorders are the most commonly tested diagnostic family on the NCMHCE. They appear in nearly every case simulation — either as a primary diagnosis, as a comorbidity, or as part of a differential. The exam's highest-yield question about this family is: **how severe, how long, and what was present before?** Those three dimensions separate the diagnoses from each other and from normal grief or adjustment.

The second key point: **the NCMHCE 2022+ format typically provides the diagnosis.** Your job is to demonstrate what comes after the diagnosis — the correct assessment sequence, the correctly prioritized interventions, the right evidence-based treatment, and the awareness of what could go wrong. This means you do not need to memorize DSM criteria for their own sake; you need to know what each diagnosis *looks like in a case* and what to do about it.

Depression questions on the exam specifically test:
1. The distinction between MDD and PDD (chronic but lower-grade)
2. The role of grief — when grief becomes MDD
3. Medical and substance rule-outs (hypothyroidism, B12, sleep disorders, alcohol)
4. The priority of safety assessment (suicidality is always rung 1)
5. The evidence base: CBT and behavioral activation for MDD; combined CBT + medication for PDD

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Key Feature | First-Line Treatment |
|-----------|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) | F32.x / F33.x | ≥5 symptoms ≥2 weeks, including depressed mood or anhedonia | CBT, behavioral activation, medication |
| Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia) | F34.1 | Depressed mood ≥2 years (adults), never symptom-free >2 months | CBT + medication (combined superior) |
| Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder | F34.8 | Severe recurrent temper outbursts + persistent irritability, children 6–18 | Multimodal (therapy + psychoeducation) |
| Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder | F32.81 | Mood symptoms in the week before menses, resolve after onset | SSRIs, lifestyle, CBT |
| Substance/Medication-Induced Depressive Disorder | F1x.14 | Temporally linked to substance use or medication | Treat underlying substance use |
| Depressive Disorder Due to Another Medical Condition | F06.31 | Etiologically linked to medical condition | Treat underlying medical condition |

### The Clinical Picture

In a case simulation, a client with **MDD** typically presents with a cluster of changes over the past two to four weeks. The narrative usually emphasizes: the *change from baseline* (this person used to function differently), the *pervasiveness* (multiple domains — sleep, appetite, concentration, social withdrawal), and the *distress or impairment*. The client may not use the word "depressed" — more often they describe feeling "numb," "empty," "like I'm going through the motions," or "just tired of everything." Tearfulness is common but not universal. Men with depression more frequently present with irritability than sadness; missing this is a common exam trap.

**Anhedonia** — the inability to feel pleasure from previously enjoyable activities — is one of the two cardinal symptoms. When a case description says "she used to love hiking but hasn't left the house in weeks," that is anhedonia.

A client with **PDD** (persistent depressive disorder) presents differently: the story is longer, more chronic, and often normalized. The client may say "I've just always been kind of a low-energy person" or "this is just how I am." They may never have experienced a symptom-free period they can remember. The depressive state feels like their personality, not an episode. This normalization of chronic depression is a key clinical marker — and it means these clients often present later and are harder to engage in treatment.

**Grief vs. MDD:** Normal grief includes waves of sadness, longing, and impaired function — but the bereaved person can typically be consoled, finds moments of positive emotion, and does not experience the pervasive anhedonia and worthlessness of MDD. The DSM-5 removed the bereavement exclusion, recognizing that grief can co-occur with or precipitate MDD. The exam expects you to know: persistent grief that meets MDD criteria for two weeks or more *is* MDD and warrants treatment.

### Differentiating Within the Family

**MDD vs. PDD:** The deciding feature is *duration*. MDD is episodic — there was a baseline the client fell from. PDD is chronic — the client has been depressed, at a lower severity, for at least two years without a two-month remission. A client can have both simultaneously ("double depression"): a PDD baseline with a current MDD episode layered on top. In the exam, "double depression" usually appears when a chronically dysthymic client suddenly deteriorates significantly.

**MDD vs. Bipolar Depression:** This distinction is critical — wrong diagnosis leads to wrong treatment (antidepressants alone can destabilize bipolar disorder). Look for: prior hypomanic or manic episodes (elevated or expansive mood, decreased need for sleep with increased energy, grandiosity, impulsivity). A thorough history is essential. The exam often tests this by presenting a depressed client with a *history* — buried in the vignette — of a period of feeling unusually energized, creative, needing little sleep, making impulsive decisions. Missing that history means missing the bipolar diagnosis.

**MDD vs. Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood:** Adjustment disorder occurs within three months of an identifiable stressor, is proportionate to the stressor, and resolves within six months of the stressor ending. If the symptoms meet MDD criteria regardless of the stressor's presence, the diagnosis is MDD.

### Assessment Tools in Context

**PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9):** Nine items, each scored 0–3. Total score 0–27.
- 0–4: minimal depression
- 5–9: mild
- 10–14: moderate
- 15–19: moderately severe
- 20–27: severe

*When to use:* initial screening, ongoing monitoring of treatment response. Note: Item 9 asks directly about suicidal ideation — a positive response requires safety assessment regardless of total score.

**Beck Depression Inventory-II (BDI-II):** 21 items. Widely used in clinical and research settings. Distinguishes cognitive (worthlessness, guilt) from somatic (sleep, appetite) symptoms.

**Hamilton Depression Rating Scale (HAM-D):** Clinician-administered (not self-report). Used to rate severity in research and inpatient contexts. Know that it is clinician-rated.

**Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS):** Not a depression measure — a suicidality measure. Always use when suicide risk is being assessed (see Chapter 17 for full treatment).

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Assessing suicidality *before* anything else when hopelessness, worthlessness, or passive death ideation is present ("what's the point," "everyone would be better off without me") — rung 1 always
- Ruling out medical causes and substances before confirming the diagnosis — thyroid, B12, sleep disorders, alcohol, steroids, beta-blockers
- Recognizing the grief-MDD distinction and treating grief-triggered MDD as MDD
- Identifying PDD's chronicity and recommending combined treatment (therapy + medication)
- Recognizing male depression may present as irritability, not sadness

**Punishes:**
- Moving to treatment without safety assessment
- Diagnosing MDD without exploring prior manic/hypomanic episodes
- Dismissing chronic dysthymia as "just personality"
- Prescribing antidepressants alone for PDD without recommending psychotherapy
- Missing the somatic presentation of depression in medical comorbidity cases

### Worked Mini-Case

*Intake vignette:*
Carla, 44, is referred by her primary care physician following a routine visit. She reports she has "never really been happy" — as long as she can remember, she has felt "kind of flat." Over the past three weeks, however, she has stopped showering, has called out of work twice, is sleeping 10+ hours but feels no more rested, and told her sister "I'm not sure I want to be here anymore."

**Reasoning chain:**

Step 1 — Safety: "Not sure I want to be here anymore" is passive suicidal ideation. This requires immediate, direct assessment before any other clinical action. *(What is the severity? Is there a plan? Intent? Means?)*

Step 2 — Differential: Carla has a long history of low-grade depression (PDD criteria: years of depressed mood) *plus* a recent acute deterioration (hypersomnia, hygiene breakdown, work absence, passive SI — MDD episode criteria). This is likely *double depression*.

Step 3 — Medical rule-out: Has her PCP already evaluated thyroid function? B12? Other metabolic causes of the acute change? The referral was from her PCP, so coordinate.

Step 4 — Treatment: Double depression requires combined treatment — CBT or IPT plus medication consultation. Behavioral activation alone is insufficient for the chronic PDD layer. The acute safety risk must be managed first (safety plan, evaluate need for higher level of care).

**Answer selection logic:** On an exam question about "first action," the answer is always the safety assessment. On a question about "appropriate treatment," the answer is combined therapy + medication with safety planning. On a question about "what complicates the prognosis," the answer is the chronic PDD substrate.

### Practice Questions

**Question 17.** A client with a confirmed MDD diagnosis has been in CBT for six weeks. She reports improvement in sleep and concentration but continues to endorse a "nagging sense that nothing matters." PHQ-9 score has dropped from 17 to 9. The counselor should:

A. Discontinue CBT and refer for medication evaluation  
B. Continue CBT and reassess; a score of 9 suggests mild residual symptoms  
C. Add a focus on behavioral activation and meaning-making  
D. Refer to a psychiatrist because medication is required for full remission  

**Correct: C.** A score of 9 represents mild, not remitted, depression. Anhedonia and loss of meaning often respond well to meaning-focused work and behavioral activation. Neither discontinuation nor mandatory medication referral is indicated at this stage. B is partially correct but doesn't offer clinical direction. C correctly names what to add.

**Question 18.** A 52-year-old man presents with fatigue, low mood, cognitive slowing, weight gain, and hair loss over four months. He attributes his symptoms to "getting older." The MOST appropriate next step is:

A. Administer the PHQ-9 and begin CBT  
B. Refer for a medical evaluation, including thyroid function tests  
C. Diagnose MDD and initiate a treatment plan  
D. Provide psychoeducation about depression and schedule the next session  

**Correct: B.** The symptom cluster — fatigue, cognitive slowing, weight gain, hair loss — is consistent with hypothyroidism. Medical rule-out (rung 2) must precede any mood disorder diagnosis. Proceeding directly to CBT or MDD diagnosis (A, C, D) before ruling out a medical cause violates the priority ladder.

**Question 19.** Which of the following presentations MOST suggests the need to assess for bipolar disorder before diagnosing and treating MDD?

A. A client who reports feeling "numb" and has lost interest in activities she previously enjoyed  
B. A client who endorses depressed mood, hypersomnia, and significant weight gain  
C. A client who reports a three-week period six years ago when she felt "on top of the world," barely slept, and made several impulsive financial decisions

**Correct: C.** The three-week period of elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, and impulsivity is a possible hypomanic or manic episode — which would indicate Bipolar I or II. A and B are classic MDD presentations without bipolar features. D suggests recurrent MDD with good treatment response, not bipolar.

**Question 20.** A client who lost her spouse eight weeks ago is unable to work, cannot stop crying, feels that life is meaningless, and endorses passive death ideation ("I just want to be with him again"). The counselor should:

A. Diagnose adjustment disorder with depressed mood, as grief is a normal response to loss  
B. Provide supportive counseling only, as medication is contraindicated in grief  
C. Assess fully for MDD and suicidality; grief-triggered depression meeting MDD criteria warrants treatment  
D. Refer for complicated grief treatment and avoid any diagnostic label during the grieving period  

**Correct: C.** The DSM-5 removed the bereavement exclusion. When grief meets MDD criteria, the diagnosis is MDD and treatment is indicated. Passive death ideation ("I want to be with him") requires direct safety assessment. A mislabels the severity. B is incorrect — medication is not contraindicated in grief-triggered MDD. D delays needed intervention.

## Chapter 7 — Anxiety Disorders

### Overview

Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental health conditions in the United States and among the most frequently appearing on the NCMHCE. The exam tests two primary skills with this family: (1) distinguishing the specific anxiety diagnoses from each other based on the *trigger and focus of the fear*, and (2) knowing the evidence-based treatment hierarchy (which always includes exposure-based work).

The central organizing principle: **anxiety disorders differ by what the person fears and what they avoid.** The feared stimulus, the physiological response, and the behavioral response (avoidance) together define the diagnosis. Knowing the feared stimulus is your key to differentiating.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Fear / Trigger | First-Line Treatment |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD) | F41.1 | Pervasive, uncontrollable worry about multiple topics | CBT, relaxation training, medication (SSRI/SNRI) |
| Panic Disorder | F41.0 | Fear of panic attacks themselves (interoceptive conditioning) | CBT with interoceptive exposure, medication |
| Social Anxiety Disorder | F40.10 | Fear of negative evaluation in social/performance situations | CBT, exposure, medication (SSRI) |
| Specific Phobia | F40.2xx | Fear of specific object/situation (heights, blood, animals, etc.) | Systematic desensitization, exposure |
| Agoraphobia | F40.00 | Fear of situations from which escape might be difficult or embarrassing | Exposure therapy, medication |
| Separation Anxiety Disorder | F93.0 | Fear of separation from attachment figures | CBT, family involvement, graduated exposure |
| Selective Mutism | F94.0 | Consistent failure to speak in specific social situations despite capability | Behavioral treatment, exposure, school collaboration |

### The Clinical Picture

**GAD** presents as a worry that does not settle — the client cannot stop, cannot turn the volume down, and has been this way for months or years. The worry shifts topics: health, finances, relationships, the future. The client often describes the worry as "out of control" and may be surprised to learn that not everyone worries this persistently. Physical symptoms include muscle tension, fatigue, irritability, sleep difficulty, and difficulty concentrating. The DSM-5 requires six months of excessive worry about multiple areas.

Exam tip: GAD clients are often high-functioning. They appear at intake because the worry has finally crossed a threshold — it is causing relationship problems, physical health effects, or occupational impairment. The chronicity of the worry (it has always been there to some degree) is key.

**Panic Disorder** is distinguished by the presence of unexpected panic attacks *plus* persistent concern about having more attacks *or* significant behavioral change to avoid them. The panic attack itself — a sudden surge of intense fear peaking within minutes — is not sufficient for the diagnosis. The disorder requires the secondary fear of the attack, or avoidance. A client who has had several panic attacks but is not particularly worried about having more and has not changed behavior does not have panic disorder.

Key exam detail: **Panic attacks can occur in multiple disorders** — they are not unique to panic disorder. A client with social anxiety disorder may have situationally triggered panic attacks in social settings. A client with PTSD may have panic attacks triggered by trauma reminders. The presence of a panic attack does not equal panic disorder.

**Social Anxiety Disorder** (formerly social phobia) is the fear of scrutiny, humiliation, or negative evaluation in social or performance situations. The client avoids situations where they might be observed, judged, or embarrassed. The exam sometimes presents a client who seems depressed (withdrawn, isolated, not engaging with others) — the key is whether the withdrawal is driven by low mood (depression) or by fear of judgment (social anxiety).

**Specific Phobia** is the most focused of the anxiety disorders — one discrete feared stimulus (or category). The exam sometimes tests the subtypes: animal, natural environment (heights, storms, water), blood-injection-injury, situational (elevators, planes), and other. The blood-injection-injury subtype is notable because it produces a *vasovagal response* — a drop in heart rate and blood pressure, sometimes leading to fainting — rather than the sympathetic arousal typical of other phobias.

### Differentiating Within the Family

**GAD vs. Depression:** Both involve fatigue, sleep problems, concentration difficulties, and dysphoria. The deciding feature: in GAD, worry is the central symptom; in depression, anhedonia and dysphoric mood are central. The client with GAD typically worries about bad things that might happen in the future; the client with depression ruminates on loss, failure, and worthlessness about the past and present.

**GAD vs. Panic Disorder:** In GAD, anxiety is chronic and diffuse (many topics, constant low-level worry). In panic disorder, anxiety is episodic and intense (discrete attacks). A client can have both.

**Social Anxiety Disorder vs. Schizoid Personality Disorder:** Both involve social withdrawal, but the mechanisms differ entirely. Schizoid PD involves genuine indifference to social relationships — the person does not want them and is not distressed by their absence. Social anxiety disorder involves a *desire* for social connection combined with *fear* of it. The client with social anxiety disorder wants to attend the party but cannot; the person with schizoid PD doesn't want to go.

**Social Anxiety Disorder vs. Avoidant Personality Disorder:** These exist on a continuum. AvPD is essentially more severe, pervasive social anxiety with greater functional impairment and a more ingrained self-view of being inadequate. The treatment is the same (CBT, exposure) but longer.

### Assessment Tools in Context

**GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale):** Seven items, scored 0–3. Scores of 5, 10, and 15 are thresholds for mild, moderate, and severe anxiety. Commonly used in primary care. Know cutoffs.

**Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI):** 21 items, self-report. Focuses on somatic and cognitive anxiety symptoms. Developed alongside the BDI-II; often used together.

**Panic Disorder Severity Scale (PDSS):** Seven items rating panic frequency, distress, anticipatory anxiety, avoidance, and impairment. Clinician-administered.

**SUDS (Subjective Units of Distress Scale):** A 0–100 rating of current distress/anxiety. Not a standalone measure — used within exposure therapy to track anxiety throughout a session.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Using exposure as the core treatment for all anxiety disorders (with systematic desensitization as a variant for specific phobias)
- Recognizing that avoidance maintains anxiety — treatment must address avoidance, not just symptoms
- Distinguishing GAD's chronicity and breadth from panic disorder's episodic intensity
- Identifying the secondary fear (fear of the attack) as what defines panic disorder, not the attack itself
- Knowing that blood-injection-injury phobia has a different physiological response (vasovagal, not sympathetic arousal) and requires adapted exposure (applied tension technique)

**Punishes:**
- Treating anxiety with relaxation alone without addressing avoidance
- Diagnosing panic disorder based on panic attacks alone without the secondary apprehension or behavioral change
- Missing comorbid depression in anxiety clients (depression very commonly co-occurs)
- Recommending prolonged benzodiazepine use as first-line (this maintains avoidance and produces dependence)
- Confusing social anxiety disorder's motivated-but-fearful presentation with schizoid PD's indifferent withdrawal

### Worked Mini-Case

*Intake vignette:*
Jordan, 28, presents describing episodes of heart racing, shortness of breath, dizziness, and a sense of unreality that come "out of nowhere" and last about ten minutes. He has had four such episodes in the past two months. Since the first episode, he has been monitoring his heart rate constantly, avoids exercise (because it raises his heart rate), and has stopped taking public transit because "I had one there once and I couldn't get off." He has visited the emergency department twice and been medically cleared each time.

**Reasoning chain:**

Step 1 — Safety/Medical: Already medically cleared twice. This is relevant — it rules out cardiac causes and supports the anxiety diagnosis.

Step 2 — Differential: Jordan has unexpected panic attacks (confirmed), persistent concern about having more attacks (heart rate monitoring), and significant behavioral changes to avoid them (no exercise, no transit). This is Panic Disorder.

Step 3 — Avoidance pattern: His avoidance has expanded (transit joins exercise). This is the hallmark of panic disorder's progressive course — without treatment, the avoidance spreads.

Step 4 — Treatment: First-line is CBT with *interoceptive exposure* — deliberately inducing panic-like sensations (through exercise, spinning, breathing exercises) in a controlled setting to break the interoceptive fear conditioning. Psychoeducation about the physiology of panic (the alarm is false; the sensations are not dangerous) is essential. Avoidance of feared situations must also be addressed with situational exposure (transit, exercise).

**Answer on treatment:** The exam will offer options including relaxation training, CBT with exposure, medication referral, and supportive counseling. The correct answer is CBT with exposure. Relaxation alone is insufficient because it does not address avoidance. Medication alone does not produce durable change without addressing avoidance.

### Practice Questions

**Question 21.** A client with GAD has been in treatment for three months. She has learned progressive muscle relaxation and diaphragmatic breathing. She reports that the exercises help "in the moment" but that the worry always comes back. The counselor should:

A. Teach additional relaxation strategies and increase practice frequency  
B. Add a cognitive component targeting the function of worry and addressing avoidance  
C. Refer for medication evaluation, as CBT has been insufficient  
D. Begin exposure therapy targeting the specific situations she worries about  

**Correct: B.** Relaxation addresses the somatic symptoms of GAD but does not address the cognitive maintenance factors (intolerance of uncertainty, metacognitive beliefs about worry, worry as avoidance of deeper feared images). The treatment is incomplete. D is tempting but is more directly applicable to phobias/panic — for GAD, the cognitive restructuring of worry beliefs and worry postponement are key additions. C is premature at three months.

**Question 22.** Which feature MOST clearly distinguishes a specific phobia diagnosis from a normal fear response?

A. The fear is disproportionate to the actual danger  
B. The feared stimulus is present and identifiable  
C. The fear causes the person to avoid the feared stimulus  
D. The fear is persistent, causes significant distress, and the avoidance or endurance causes functional impairment  

**Correct: D.** Many fears are disproportionate (A) and many people avoid things they're scared of (C) — neither alone makes a diagnosis. The distinguishing features are: the persistence, the significant distress, and the functional impairment. B describes a trigger, not a diagnostic criterion.

**Question 23.** A client reports severe fear and avoidance of blood draws, to the point that she has avoided necessary medical care for two years. She describes that when exposed to needles or blood, she initially feels panicky but then becomes lightheaded and has fainted twice. The counselor should:

A. Apply standard systematic desensitization, as this is a specific phobia  
B. Use applied tension technique, as the vasovagal response requires a different approach  
C. Refer to a physician first, as fainting suggests a medical issue  
D. Treat with interoceptive exposure, as this presentation resembles panic disorder  

**Correct: B.** Blood-injection-injury phobia produces a vasovagal (fainting) response rather than the sympathetic arousal typical of anxiety. Standard exposure would drive the heart rate up and then allow the vasovagal drop — potentially reinforcing fainting. Applied tension (tensing large muscle groups to maintain blood pressure) is the evidence-based adaptation. C is not needed — vasovagal fainting in this context is a known response to the phobia. D is incorrect — this is not panic disorder.

She also reports that she frequently lies awake at night and feels "keyed up" throughout the day. The MOST likely diagnosis is:

A. MDD  
B. Panic Disorder  
C. GAD  
D. OCD  

**Correct: C.** Three years of pervasive, uncontrollable worry about multiple domains, with insomnia and hyperarousal, is the clinical picture of GAD. MDD (A) would feature anhedonia and depressed mood as the central feature. Panic disorder (B) involves episodic attacks. OCD (D) involves intrusive obsessions and compulsions, not general worry.

## Chapter 8 — Trauma and Stressor-Related Disorders

### Overview

The trauma chapter is among the highest-yield diagnostic chapters on the NCMHCE — not because PTSD is complicated to identify, but because the exam tests the nuances: subthreshold presentations, the difference between acute stress disorder and PTSD, the distinction between PTSD and adjustment disorder, complex trauma presentations, and the critical treatment hierarchy.

The non-negotiable clinical principle: **trauma clients require safety and stabilization before any processing work begins.** Moving to trauma memory processing before the client has adequate distress tolerance skills, a stable therapeutic alliance, and a safe living environment is a clinical error that causes harm. The exam will present scenarios where the wrong answer is "begin trauma processing immediately" — always stabilize first.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Key Feature | Duration | First-Line Treatment |
|-----------|--------|-------------|---------|---------------------|
| PTSD | F43.10 | Re-experiencing, avoidance, negative cognitions/mood, hyperarousal following Criterion A trauma | >1 month | Trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT), EMDR, CPT, PE |
| Acute Stress Disorder | F43.0 | Same symptom domains as PTSD | 3 days – 1 month post-trauma | Stabilization, CBT, crisis support |
| Adjustment Disorder | F43.2x | Emotional/behavioral symptoms in response to stressor; does not meet criteria for another disorder | Within 3 months of stressor; resolves within 6 months | Supportive therapy, brief CBT |
| Reactive Attachment Disorder | F94.1 | Disturbed social relatedness in children following inadequate caregiving | Early childhood | Caregiver-focused intervention, dyadic therapy |
| Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder | F94.2 | Indiscriminate attachment in children; approaches strangers with little hesitation | Early childhood | Caregiver stability, attachment-focused work |

### The Clinical Picture

**PTSD** presents across four symptom clusters. Understanding these helps you identify PTSD in a case sim where the diagnosis isn't named:

1. **Re-experiencing (intrusion):** Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories, psychological distress when reminded of the trauma. The client "goes back" — not just remembers, but *re-lives*.
2. **Avoidance:** Avoids trauma-related thoughts, feelings, people, places, situations, conversations. The avoidance maintains the disorder by preventing natural processing.
3. **Negative alterations in cognition and mood:** Distorted blame, persistent negative emotions, diminished interest, feeling detached from others, inability to experience positive emotions ("emotional numbing"), persistent negative beliefs ("the world is dangerous," "I am permanently damaged").
4. **Hyperarousal and reactivity:** Hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, sleep disturbance, irritability, reckless or aggressive behavior, concentration problems.

The exam sometimes presents PTSD in non-stereotypical ways. A combat veteran is the clichéd presentation — but PTSD also follows childhood abuse, sexual assault, medical trauma, accidents, witnessing violence, and workplace incidents. The Criterion A trauma does not have to be experienced directly — it can be witnessed or learned about (if it affects a close person).

**Complex PTSD** (not in DSM-5 as a standalone diagnosis, but recognized in ICD-11 and clinically important): results from prolonged, repeated trauma (childhood abuse, domestic violence, captivity). Presents with affect dysregulation, dissociation, disrupted self-perception, disturbed relationships, and loss of systems of meaning *in addition to* core PTSD symptoms. Treatment must include a sustained stabilization phase before any exposure-based work.

**Acute Stress Disorder** is PTSD's time-limited precursor — the same symptoms, but within the first month. ASD predicts later PTSD. Early intervention (trauma-focused CBT within the acute phase) can reduce PTSD incidence.

### Differentiating Within the Family

**PTSD vs. Adjustment Disorder:** Both follow a stressor. The key distinction: PTSD requires a Criterion A traumatic stressor (actual or threatened death, serious injury, sexual violence) and specific symptom clusters (re-experiencing, avoidance, negative cognitions, hyperarousal). Adjustment disorder occurs after a stressor of *any* type (job loss, divorce, moving) and doesn't require those specific symptom clusters. Adjustment disorder is, in effect, a clinical response to a stressor that does not meet criteria for any other disorder.

**PTSD vs. MDD:** Depression and PTSD commonly co-occur. The differentiating features: PTSD has re-experiencing (flashbacks, nightmares), avoidance of trauma-related cues, and hyperarousal — none of which are cardinal MDD features. When both are present, both are diagnosed (comorbidity).

**PTSD vs. Dissociative Disorders:** Dissociation can occur in PTSD (as a symptom within the negative alterations cluster or as the dissociative subtype of PTSD). The dissociative subtype of PTSD presents with depersonalization and derealization alongside PTSD symptoms. True dissociative disorders (DID, depersonalization/derealization disorder) are distinct entities (see Chapter 20).

### Assessment Tools in Context

**PCL-5 (PTSD Checklist for DSM-5):** 20-item self-report measure aligned with DSM-5 criteria. Frequently used for screening and monitoring treatment response. Scores ≥31–33 suggest probable PTSD (cutoff varies by context). Each item maps to a DSM-5 symptom — clinically useful for tracking specific clusters.

**CAPS-5 (Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale):** The gold standard — a structured clinician interview that assesses PTSD diagnosis and severity. Used in research and for formal diagnosis. Know that it is *clinician-administered*.

**Trauma Symptom Inventory (TSI):** Broader trauma-related assessment including depression, anxiety, anger, dissociation, sexual concerns. Used when complex trauma is suspected.

**ACE Score (Adverse Childhood Experiences):** Not a diagnostic tool — a cumulative risk indicator based on 10 categories of childhood adversity. Higher scores correlate with more severe adult health and mental health outcomes. Used in psychoeducation and treatment planning.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- The three-phase treatment model: (1) safety and stabilization, (2) trauma memory processing, (3) reintegration — never skip phase 1
- Evidence-based trauma treatments: TF-CBT (children), CPT (cognitive processing therapy), PE (prolonged exposure), EMDR
- Direct but compassionate safety assessment when suicidality or self-harm is present
- Recognizing that avoidance is central — treatment must approach what the client is avoiding
- Cultural sensitivity around trauma disclosure (especially for sexual trauma or politically-motivated trauma)

**Punishes:**
- Beginning trauma memory processing (exposure, EMDR) before stabilization
- Forcing trauma disclosure before trust is established
- Missing that PTSD and MDD, or PTSD and substance use disorder, commonly co-occur and both require treatment
- Diagnosing adjustment disorder when the stressor meets Criterion A and symptoms meet PTSD criteria
- Treating ASD with watchful waiting only when early CBT can reduce PTSD risk

### Worked Mini-Case

*Case vignette:*
Diane, 34, is referred following a car accident eight weeks ago in which she was not injured but witnessed another driver's death. She reports nightmares about the crash nightly, avoids driving (her husband takes her to work), becomes distressed when she sees news coverage of accidents, and has been "on edge" at all times since the accident. She endorses feeling detached from her family and reports she "can't feel anything." Her husband reports she has become irritable and startles easily at sounds.

**Reasoning chain:**

Step 1 — Criterion A: Diane witnessed another person's death. This meets Criterion A (witnessed death).

Step 2 — Symptom clusters:
- Re-experiencing: nightmares ✓
- Avoidance: avoids driving, avoids accident news coverage ✓
- Negative alterations in cognition/mood: emotional numbing, detachment from family ✓
- Hyperarousal: on edge, irritability, exaggerated startle ✓

Step 3 — Duration: Eight weeks (>1 month) → PTSD, not ASD.

Step 4 — Safety: Brief assessment — no SI mentioned, but irritability and numbing warrant asking about self-harm.

Step 5 — Treatment: Begin with Phase 1 (stabilization, psychoeducation about PTSD, grounding skills, safety). Then CPT or PE to address the trauma memory and avoidance. Involve the husband (he is already a support resource).

**Key exam point:** The exam might present this and ask whether driving avoidance should be "respected" (normalized and left in place) vs. addressed. The correct answer: avoidance is a maintaining factor; treatment must eventually address it. Permanently allowing the avoidance = maintaining the disorder.

### Practice Questions

**Question 25.** A client with PTSD and co-occurring alcohol use disorder (she drinks to suppress nightmares) is beginning outpatient treatment. The counselor should FIRST:

A. Begin prolonged exposure to the trauma memories while simultaneously addressing the alcohol use

Initiating prolonged exposure while the client is actively drinking is contraindicated — the client lacks the distress tolerance and sobriety needed to process traumatic material safely. C over-sequences treatment — integrated (dual) treatment is more effective than sequential. D ignores the substance use, which is a primary maintaining factor.

## Chapter 9 — Substance-Related and Addictive Disorders

### Overview

Substance use disorders occupy a unique position on the NCMHCE because they appear in two ways: as primary diagnoses, and as co-occurring conditions that complicate every other disorder. The exam tests whether you can (1) recognize the difference between use, misuse, and disorder; (2) apply the diagnostic criteria correctly; (3) identify the appropriate level of care; and (4) avoid the two classic errors — over-pathologizing normal use and under-recognizing serious use disorders in high-functioning clients.

The single most testable substance question: **what is the appropriate first clinical action with a new client who reports substance use?** The answer is: *assess the severity and functional impact before doing anything else.* Do not assume use = disorder, and do not assume the client knows whether they have a problem.

The second key concept: **co-occurring (dual) disorders require integrated treatment.** Treating only the mental health issue while ignoring the substance use, or treating only the substance use while ignoring the mental health issue, produces consistently worse outcomes than treating both simultaneously.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Severity | Key Criteria |
|-----------|--------|----------|-------------|
| Alcohol Use Disorder (mild/moderate/severe) | F10.10/.20/.20 | 2–3 Sx: mild; 4–5: moderate; ≥6: severe | ≥2 of 11 criteria in 12 months |
| Cannabis Use Disorder | F12.10 | Same 11-criterion scale | Includes withdrawal (irritability, sleep, appetite) |
| Opioid Use Disorder | F11.10 | Same scale | Tolerance, withdrawal, compulsive use despite consequences |
| Stimulant Use Disorder | F15.10 | Same scale | Cocaine, amphetamine, methamphetamine |
| Sedative/Hypnotic/Anxiolytic Use Disorder | F13.10 | Same scale | Includes benzodiazepines |
| Gambling Disorder | F63.0 | Only behavioral (non-substance) addictive disorder in DSM-5 | Persistent, problematic gambling with 4+ of 9 criteria |

**The 11 diagnostic criteria for substance use disorders** (applied to each substance):
1. Taking larger amounts or longer than intended
2. Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down
3. Great deal of time spent obtaining, using, or recovering
4. Craving
5. Failure to fulfill major role obligations
6. Continued use despite social/interpersonal problems
7. Important activities given up
8. Use in hazardous situations
9. Continued use despite knowledge of physical/psychological problems caused by it
10. Tolerance (needing more for same effect)
11. Withdrawal

*Tolerance and withdrawal are criteria 10 and 11 — they do not automatically indicate a substance use disorder when they occur in the context of medically prescribed use.*

### The Clinical Picture

The clinical picture of a substance use disorder often conceals itself. High-functioning individuals with moderate or severe alcohol use disorder may appear polished and organized. Cannabis use disorder clients may resist the diagnosis ("it's legal now"). Opioid use disorder often presents first in medical settings.

In a case simulation, look for:
- **The erosion pattern:** things the person used to do that they no longer do (work performance declining, relationships deteriorating, hobbies abandoned)
- **The explaining-it-away pattern:** the client attributes every problem to something other than the substance ("my boss is terrible," "my marriage was already struggling")
- **The all-or-nothing disclosure:** the client's first report minimizes; the full picture emerges only with direct, non-judgmental questions

**Motivational interviewing (MI)** is the evidence-based approach for the pre-contemplation and contemplation stages. MI does not argue with the client, does not lecture, and does not try to convince. It explores ambivalence (the "yes, but" of addiction) through open questions, reflections, affirmations, and summaries — the OARS skills.

**FRAMES model** (Brief Intervention context):
- **F**eedback (personalized)
- **R**esponsibility (emphasize client's own choice)
- **A**dvice (clear recommendation)
- **M**enu of options
- **E**mpathy
- **S**elf-efficacy support

### The ASAM Levels of Care

The American Society of Addiction Medicine (ASAM) criteria guide placement in addiction treatment. Know the levels:

| Level | Name | Setting |
|-------|------|---------|
| 0.5 | Early Intervention | Brief outpatient |
| 1 | Outpatient Services | <9 hours/week |
| 2.1 | Intensive Outpatient (IOP) | 9–19 hours/week |
| 2.5 | Partial Hospitalization (PHP) | ≥20 hours/week |
| 3.1–3.7 | Residential | 24-hour therapeutic community |
| 4 | Medically Managed Intensive Inpatient | 24-hour medical supervision |

The exam expects you to match severity to level of care. A client with moderate alcohol use disorder and no medical complications goes to outpatient or IOP, not residential. A client with severe opioid use disorder and current withdrawal with medical instability goes to level 4.

### Assessment Tools in Context

**AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test):** 10 items. Total score 0–40.
- 8–15: simple advice
- 16–19: brief counseling + monitoring
- ≥20: referral for evaluation

**AUDIT-C:** 3-item abbreviated screen. Scores ≥4 (men) or ≥3 (women) suggest likely AUD.

**CAGE:** 4-item clinical screen (Cut down / Annoyed / Guilty / Eye-opener). ≥2 positive responses = likely AUD. Fast and memorable; less sensitive than AUDIT for lower-severity use.

**DAST-10 (Drug Abuse Screening Test):** 10-item screen for non-alcohol drug use. Score ≥3 suggests need for further assessment.

**ASI (Addiction Severity Index):** Comprehensive structured interview across seven domains (medical, employment, alcohol, drugs, legal, family/social, psychiatric). Used for treatment planning, not just screening.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Matching level of care to ASAM criteria (not defaulting to outpatient for all presentations)
- Using MI (not confrontation or lectures) with ambivalent clients in pre-contemplation or contemplation
- Recognizing the need to assess both substance use AND mental health simultaneously
- Assessing for withdrawal safety — alcohol and benzodiazepine withdrawal can be life-threatening (seizures, delirium tremens); opioid withdrawal is medically uncomfortable but rarely fatal
- Integrated treatment for co-occurring disorders, not sequential

**Punishes:**
- Confronting denial without using MI
- Diagnosing a substance use disorder without systematic assessment (AUDIT, CAGE, clinical interview)
- Ignoring the substance use when treating another primary disorder
- Sending all clients to residential regardless of severity
- Missing alcohol withdrawal risk in clients who present with co-occurring medical issues

### Worked Mini-Case

*Intake vignette:*
Robert, 47, is self-referred. He says, "My wife thinks I drink too much, but I can handle it." He reports drinking "six or seven" beers most evenings ("maybe more on weekends"). He denies problems at work — he is a high-functioning attorney. He reports that he tried to stop drinking for two weeks last year and experienced sweats, tremors, and difficulty sleeping. He stopped the attempt. He endorses some morning drinking to "feel right."

**Reasoning chain:**

Step 1 — Assess severity: Robert endorses 4–5 of the 11 DSM criteria. Morning drinking (using in situations where it's not customary), attempted withdrawal with physiological symptoms, drinking larger amounts than described (self-minimization), wife noticing the problem, and the failed quit attempt. This is likely **moderate to severe AUD**.

Step 2 — Medical priority: The history of tremors and sweating during the prior cessation attempt signals **withdrawal risk**. Alcohol withdrawal can be medically dangerous (seizures, delirium tremens). Before outpatient treatment begins, a medical evaluation and possibly medical management of withdrawal is needed (rung 2 on the priority ladder).

Step 3 — Motivational stance: Robert is in pre-contemplation ("I can handle it"). MI is the indicated approach. Confronting denial will increase resistance. Reflect ambivalence: "Your wife is concerned, you tried to stop once — what's your own take on your drinking?"

Step 4 — Level of care: Given severity and withdrawal history, outpatient is not safe until medically stabilized. Consider medically supervised detox (level 3.7 or 4) followed by residential or PHP.

**Exam answer:** The first step is medical evaluation (rung 2) before any psychosocial treatment begins.

**Correct: B.** MI explores ambivalence — the client's own perceived benefits and costs — without imposing the counselor's agenda. The change talk that emerges from this exploration is more durable than change that comes from being lectured (A, C) or pressured (D).

## Chapter 10 — Personality Disorders

### Overview

Personality disorders are among the most challenging chapters on the NCMHCE — not because the diagnosis is hard to recognize, but because the exam tests your understanding of *how personality disorders change the clinical picture* and what adaptations they require in the therapeutic relationship.

The foundational principle: a personality disorder is a **persistent, pervasive, inflexible pattern** of experiencing and relating to the world that causes distress or impairment. The key word is *pervasive* — the pattern appears across multiple contexts (work, relationships, the counseling relationship itself), is stable over time, and is ego-syntonic (the client experiences the pattern as "just who I am," not as symptoms to be treated). This ego-syntonic quality is why personality disorder clients often do not present seeking change in their personality — they present because the world keeps responding badly to them.

For exam purposes, know the clusters, know the highest-tested diagnoses (Borderline, Narcissistic, Antisocial), and know what DBT targets and why.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

**Cluster A — "Odd/Eccentric"**

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Paranoid PD | F60.0 | Pervasive distrust and suspicion |
| Schizoid PD | F60.1 | Detachment from social relationships; restricted affect; indifferent to others |
| Schizotypal PD | F21 | Social isolation + odd beliefs, perceptions, speech, and behavior |

**Cluster B — "Dramatic/Emotional/Erratic"**

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Antisocial PD | F60.2 | Pervasive disregard for and violation of others' rights (≥18; conduct disorder before 15) |
| Borderline PD | F60.3 | Instability in relationships, self-image, affect, and impulsivity |
| Histrionic PD | F60.4 | Excessive emotionality and attention-seeking |
| Narcissistic PD | F60.81 | Grandiosity, need for admiration, lack of empathy |

**Cluster C — "Anxious/Fearful"**

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Core Pattern |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Avoidant PD | F60.6 | Social inhibition, feelings of inadequacy, hypersensitivity to negative evaluation |
| Dependent PD | F60.7 | Excessive need to be taken care of; submissiveness; fear of separation |
| Obsessive-Compulsive PD | F60.5 | Preoccupation with orderliness, perfectionism, control (distinct from OCD) |

### The Clinical Picture

**Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD)** is the highest-yield personality disorder on the exam. The clinical picture: a client whose relationships are intense and unstable (idealization followed by devaluation), who experiences profound fear of abandonment (real or imagined), who has a markedly unstable self-image, who engages in impulsive behaviors that are self-damaging (spending, sex, substance use, self-harm), and whose mood shifts rapidly. The counseling relationship itself will often reproduce the BPD relational pattern — the client may idealize the counselor early and devalue them sharply after a perceived abandonment (e.g., a canceled session or a moment when the counselor seems less attuned).

*In the counseling relationship:* A client with BPD will test the therapeutic frame (late cancellations, frequent between-session contact, testing limits), will react intensely to breaks and endings, and may oscillate between idealization ("You're the only person who's ever understood me") and devaluation ("You're just like everyone else — you don't care"). The exam expects you to: maintain a consistent frame, respond with validation rather than defensiveness, and use DBT skills-based interventions.

**Narcissistic PD** in therapy: the client presents with a sense of entitlement, often expects special treatment (phone the counselor directly, session times accommodated to their schedule), becomes irritable or contemptuous when the counselor doesn't agree or provide validation. Beneath the grandiosity is often profound shame. The clinical task is to neither attack the grandiosity nor collude with it.

**Antisocial PD** and the limits of individual therapy: clients with ASPD have limited capacity for guilt, remorse, and empathy. Individual talk therapy has limited evidence for ASPD. Court-mandated treatment is common; motivation is typically external. The exam sometimes tests whether you would maintain treatment with a client who is actively deceiving you — the answer involves: monitor honesty, maintain the therapeutic frame, and be realistic about the goals of treatment.

### Differentiating Within the Family

**BPD vs. Bipolar II:** The most common diagnostic confusion. Both involve mood instability, impulsivity, and interpersonal difficulties.

| Feature | BPD | Bipolar II |
|---------|-----|-----------|
| Mood episode duration | Hours to days, reactive to events | Days to weeks, not necessarily reactive |
| Trigger | Often interpersonal (abandonment, rejection) | May be autonomous |
| Self-harm | Common | Less central |
| Identity disturbance | Core feature | Not a feature |
| Hypomanic episodes | Not part of BPD | Defining feature of Bipolar II |

**Schizoid PD vs. Avoidant PD:** Both involve social withdrawal. The distinction: Schizoid PD is withdrawal *by preference* (the person genuinely does not want relationships, feels no distress about isolation). Avoidant PD is withdrawal *by fear* (the person desperately wants relationships but is too afraid of rejection to pursue them).

**OCPD vs. OCD:** OCPD is ego-syntonic — the person sees their orderliness, perfectionism, and control as virtues, not symptoms. OCD is ego-dystonic — the obsessions and compulsions are experienced as unwanted and distressing. A person with OCPD does not experience their rigidity as a problem; a person with OCD wishes they could stop the compulsions.

### Assessment Tools in Context

**Personality disorder diagnosis** is made through clinical interview (comprehensive history, collateral sources) — there is no brief screening tool that reliably diagnoses a PD. The MMPI-2 (Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory) and MCMI-IV (Millon Clinical Multiaxial Inventory) are the most commonly used structured assessment tools. The exam expects you to know their purpose (comprehensive personality assessment) without needing to know specific scales in depth.

**McLean Screening Instrument for BPD (MSI-BPD):** 10-item self-report, used to screen specifically for BPD. A score ≥7 suggests the need for further evaluation.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- DBT as the first-line evidence-based treatment for BPD (developed specifically for this population)
- Maintaining the therapeutic frame consistently with personality disorder clients (consistency is therapeutic)
- Validating the client's emotional experience before addressing behavior
- Recognizing that personality disorder traits can intensify in times of stress
- Understanding that personality disorders are ego-syntonic and that the client often does not identify the PD pattern as the problem

**Punishes:**
- Diagnosing PD in a first session (diagnosis requires pervasive, persistent pattern across contexts and time)
- Diagnosing PD under the age of 18 (except ASPD's precursor, conduct disorder) — PD diagnosis in adolescents is generally deferred
- Colluding with splitting in BPD by taking sides in the client's devaluation of others
- Using generic supportive therapy for BPD when DBT is available
- Ignoring the client's expressed suicidal ideation in BPD because "it's chronic" — chronic suicidality requires ongoing assessment; each expression must be evaluated

### Worked Mini-Case

*Intake vignette:*
Sandra, 26, presents for the third time in 18 months. She was discharged from a prior counselor ("she stopped getting me") and another before that ("he was useless from the start"). She describes cutting on her arms "when things get too intense" and has had two brief psychiatric hospitalizations following "fights with people I trusted." She says you are "the only one who might actually help" and wants to meet three times per week.

**Reasoning chain:**

Step 1 — Safety: Self-harm is present. Assess current ideation, plan, intent. Distinguish non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) from suicidal self-harm — in BPD, cutting frequently serves an affect-regulation function (not suicidal intent), but this must always be verified.

Step 2 — Diagnostic picture: The pattern across three counselors (splitting, idealization-devaluation), self-harm, hospitalizations after intense interpersonal conflict, and the immediate idealization ("the only one who might actually help") is a classic BPD presentation.

Step 3 — Frame-setting: Begin with a clear therapeutic agreement. Number of sessions (three times per week is typically not the standard outpatient frame; discuss what's appropriate and sustainable). Crisis plan (what Sandra does before calling the counselor or going to the ER). Limit-setting communicated warmly.

Step 4 — Treatment: DBT — skills training in distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness, and mindfulness. The self-harm requires a behavioral chain analysis to understand its function and to develop alternatives.

Step 5 — Counter-transference: "The only one who might actually help" is an idealization that, if accepted uncritically, will be followed by devaluation. The counselor should receive it warmly but not collude with it.

## Chapter 11 — Bipolar and Related Disorders

### Overview

Bipolar disorders are among the most serious — and most misdiagnosed — conditions the NCMHCE tests. The exam's highest concern: **missing a manic or hypomanic history when a client presents in a depressive episode.** Prescribing an antidepressant to someone with unrecognized bipolar disorder can precipitate a manic episode. The exam rewards thoroughness — always take a mood episode history, not just a current symptom snapshot.

The second critical concept: **mania and hypomania differ in severity and impact, not in kind.** Hypomania is a lighter, shorter, and less impairing version of mania. Understanding this distinction separates Bipolar I from Bipolar II.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Defining Feature | First-Line Treatment |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Bipolar I | F31.x | At least one manic episode (with or without depression) | Mood stabilizer (lithium, valproate), psychotherapy adjunct |
| Bipolar II | F31.81 | At least one hypomanic episode + at least one MDD episode; NO full mania | Mood stabilizer, lamotrigine; psychotherapy |
| Cyclothymic Disorder | F34.0 | ≥2 years of hypomanic and depressive symptoms; never meeting full criteria for either | Mood stabilizer, psychotherapy |
| Substance/Medication-Induced Bipolar | F1x.x4 | Manic symptoms during or shortly after substance use/withdrawal | Treat underlying cause |
| Bipolar Due to Medical Condition | F06.33 | Etiologically linked to medical condition | Treat underlying cause |

### The Clinical Picture

**Mania** (Bipolar I): A distinct period of abnormally elevated, expansive, or irritable mood *plus* increased goal-directed activity or energy, lasting at least one week (or any duration if hospitalization required). During this period, three or more of: grandiosity, decreased need for sleep (feels rested after 3 hours), pressured speech, racing thoughts, distractibility, increased goal-directed activity, and reckless behavior. The key marker: the change is *distinct from baseline* and represents a *different level of functioning*. The client who is always ambitious and energetic is not manic. The client who has gone three days without sleep, maxed out four credit cards, and started four new business ventures simultaneously — that is mania.

**Hypomania** (Bipolar II): The same symptom criteria, but lasting at least four consecutive days, not severe enough to require hospitalization, and without psychotic features. The crucial point: **the person feels great during hypomania.** They are productive, creative, charming, and energized. They often do not identify hypomania as a problem — they come in depressed and, when asked about their "high" periods, describe them as their best functioning. This is why hypomanic episodes are missed.

Exam trap: A client presents with MDD. You review the history and she says, "Actually, there are periods — usually in the spring — when I need less sleep and I get a lot done. I feel fantastic during those times." This is a hypomanic episode. The diagnosis is Bipolar II, not MDD. The treatment changes significantly.

### Differentiating Within the Family

**Bipolar I vs. Bipolar II:** The single distinguishing feature is whether a full manic episode has occurred. Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode (may or may not have depressive episodes). Bipolar II requires at least one hypomanic episode and at least one major depressive episode — and *never* a full manic episode. A Bipolar II client who later develops a full manic episode becomes Bipolar I.

**Bipolar II vs. Cyclothymia:** Cyclothymia involves chronic, fluctuating mood over ≥2 years, with periods of hypomanic symptoms and periods of depressive symptoms that *never meet full criteria* for either a hypomanic episode or an MDD episode. It is milder but chronically unstable. Cyclothymia in adolescents is a risk factor for developing full Bipolar I or II.

**BPD vs. Bipolar II:** (See Chapter 10 table.) The critical distinguishing feature is that Bipolar II has discrete episodes (days to weeks) that are relatively autonomous from triggers; BPD mood shifts are reactive to events (hours to days), interpersonally triggered, and accompanied by identity disturbance and self-harm.

### What the Exam Rewards / What It Punishes

**Rewards:**
- Taking a full mood history before diagnosing or treating depression (rule out prior manic/hypomanic episodes)
- Recognizing that hypomania is often ego-syntonic and not spontaneously reported
- Recommending mood stabilizer (not antidepressant monotherapy) as primary pharmacological treatment
- Psychotherapy as an adjunct (CBT, IPSRT — interpersonal and social rhythm therapy — are evidence-based)
- Psychoeducation about sleep regulation as a critical component of bipolar management

**Punishes:**
- Diagnosing and treating MDD without inquiring about prior elevated mood periods
- Recommending antidepressant monotherapy for a bipolar client (can precipitate mania)
- Confusing BPD mood instability with hypomanic episodes
- Failing to assess for psychosis during manic episodes (Bipolar I with psychotic features requires a different care level)

B. Ask about any prior periods of elevated mood, decreased need for sleep, increased energy, or impulsive behavior

**Correct: B.** Screening for prior hypomanic or manic episodes is necessary before confirming MDD. If the client has had hypomanic episodes, the diagnosis is Bipolar II — with different treatment implications. CBT (D) is not appropriate for bipolar depression without mood stabilization.

C. Remind her that Bipolar I is a lifelong condition requiring permanent medication  
D. Refer back to her psychiatrist and defer the discussion  

**Correct: B.** Explore before educating — but psychoeducation is clearly indicated here. Medication discontinuation in Bipolar I dramatically increases relapse risk. This is a collaborative conversation that includes both the counselor and the prescriber. A is too permissive without addressing the risk. C is directive without exploration. D is avoidant (the counselor should engage).

## Chapter 12 — Neurodevelopmental Disorders

### Overview

Neurodevelopmental disorders are characterized by developmental deficits that emerge early in life. On the NCMHCE, the exam primarily tests: recognition of ADHD presentations across the lifespan, awareness of ASD's clinical picture in functioning adults, appropriate use of psychological testing, and the distinction between intellectual disability and learning disorders.

### Diagnosis Reference Table

| Diagnosis | ICD-10 | Key Feature | First-Line Treatment |
|-----------|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| ADHD — Combined | F90.2 | ≥6 inattentive + ≥6 hyperactive/impulsive symptoms, present in ≥2 settings | Multimodal: behavioral, medication (stimulants), psychoeducation |
| ADHD — Predominantly Inattentive | F90.0 | ≥6 inattentive symptoms | Same multimodal approach |
| ADHD — Predominantly Hyperactive/Impulsive | F90.1 | ≥6 hyperactive/impulsive symptoms | Same multimodal approach |
| Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) | F84.0 | Persistent deficits in social communication + restricted/repetitive behaviors | ABA (behavioral), speech, OT, social skills; no cure |
| Intellectual Disability | F7x | Significant limitations in intellectual functioning AND adaptive behavior | Skills-based support, educational planning, family psychoeducation |
| Specific Learning Disorder | F81.x | With impairment in reading (dyslexia), writing (dysgraphia), or math (dyscalculia) | Specialized instruction, accommodations |
| Developmental Coordination Disorder | F82 | Marked impairment in motor skills | Occupational therapy |
| Tourette's Disorder | F95.2 | Multiple motor + ≥1 vocal tic, present >1 year | Behavioral (CBIT), medication if needed |

### The Clinical Picture

**ADHD in adults:** The presenting complaint is rarely "I can't sit still" — it is more often: "I can't finish anything," "I'm always late," "I lose things constantly," "I start projects and never complete them." Adults with ADHD often develop compensatory strategies that mask symptoms; the disorder comes to attention when demands increase (new job, graduate school, parenthood). The exam expects you to know that ADHD diagnosis in adults requires evidence that symptoms were present before age 12 — not just that they are present now.

**ASD in higher-functioning adults:** These clients may present for anxiety, depression, or relationship difficulties — not for autism. The ASD features become apparent in the clinical relationship: unusual communication style, difficulty with reciprocity, literal interpretation of figurative language, intense interests, rigidity around routines, and sensory sensitivities. The exam may test whether you recognize ASD features in a client who does not carry the diagnosis.

### Assessment Tools in Context

**Conners Rating Scales:** Parent and teacher rating forms for ADHD; multiple informants are essential because ADHD must be present in more than one setting.

**ADOS-2 (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule):** Structured observational assessment for ASD; clinician-administered. Gold standard for ASD diagnosis.

**WAIS-IV (Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale):** Administered by a licensed psychologist; provides IQ scores across verbal comprehension, perceptual reasoning, working memory, and processing speed. Used in ID diagnosis.

### Practice Questions

**Question 39.** A 28-year-old client is referred after her employer cited repeated missed deadlines and organizational failures. She reports that she has "always been scattered" but managed through college with extra time accommodations. She meets criteria for ADHD — predominantly inattentive. The counselor's MOST appropriate first step in treatment planning is:

A. Refer immediately for a stimulant medication evaluation  
B. Provide psychoeducation, assess for comorbidities (anxiety, depression, sleep), and develop a multimodal plan  
C. Begin CBT focused on distorted cognitions about productivity  
D. Recommend time management coaching as a standalone intervention  

**Correct: B.** Psychoeducation and comprehensive assessment are the first steps in multimodal treatment planning for adult ADHD. Medication evaluation (A) is often appropriate but not necessarily the *first* step. CBT alone (C) is a component, not a complete plan. Coaching (D) is helpful but not a standalone evidence-based treatment.

**Question 40.** Which of the following behaviors in a 5-year-old child is MOST consistent with an ASD diagnosis?

A. Difficulty sharing toys with siblings  
B. Repetitive, stereotyped hand-flapping when excited, limited eye contact, and speaking in scripts from memorized TV shows  
C. Hyperactivity and impulsivity in the classroom and at home  
D. Selective mutism in school but normal communication at home  

**Correct: B.** Hand-flapping (stereotyped behavior), reduced eye contact (social communication deficit), and echolalia (scripted speech) are core ASD features. A is developmentally normative. C is more consistent with ADHD. D is selective mutism.
